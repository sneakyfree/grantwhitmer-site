The Approach

Fundamentals win

Grant has spent his life at the fundamentals of hard disciplines — he coached wrestling at the Division I level, competed as an athlete to the Olympic Team Trials, and has played violin and guitar since he was a boy. Every one of them taught the same lesson: technique matters, attitude matters, work ethic matters — but fundamentals are king.

A great coach once put it plainly: in every skill, you are either getting better or getting worse — there is a maintenance tax. An instrument is no different. A sport is no different. And conducting intelligence is no different. If you want to be elite at it, you don't cram it — you practice it, a little, every day.

So that is what Grant coaches: not tricks, but the fundamentals — and they all center on one thing. The oldest and greatest human superpower: the Socratic method, the ability to formulate and ask the question. The right question opens the floodgates — it draws out the combined creativity, knowledge, and wisdom of all of human history, distilled into the weights of the AI, and puts that whole "team" to work for you. Master it, and you can finally turn the visions you have carried in your mind your whole life into something real.

"In every skill, you're either getting better or getting worse. There is a maintenance tax. So you practice — every day."
— Grant Whitmer
Fundamental I

Think in questions, not commands. The Socratic method is the whole game — the right question beats the right instruction every time.

Fundamental II

Practice every day. Mind the maintenance tax. Like a violin or a sport, the skill is always sharpening or dulling — never still.

Fundamental III

Open the floodgates. Ask the question that unlocks the genius of the whole team — all of humanity's knowledge, baked into the machine.

The Craft

Conducting intelligence

When you sit down to work with an AI, you are a child monarch who just inherited a kingdom — seated at a table of advisors who each know a thousand times what you know. You will never out-think them. But you can learn to conduct them.

And the gap only widens. Today we are child monarchs. Soon we'll be infant monarchs; not long after, monarchs still in the womb — a chimpanzee with a meat computer, at the head of a table of giants. When the difference in raw intelligence grows that large, there is exactly one thing left worth getting extraordinarily good at: conducting the intelligence in the room.

Here's the miracle most people miss: right now, architecturally, humanity holds a superpower these machines simply do not have. We can ask questions. Power on an LLM and never ask it anything — never even task it to ask on its own — and it will sit there humming, waiting, until the power runs out and it goes dark. It cannot generate a single question unprompted. A five-year-old, thirty seconds into a movie, asks twenty. Unbidden. Ours is the only animal in the kingdom that can.

"We conducted with our voices for hundreds of thousands of years before the keyboard — a 150-year-old relic built to stop typewriter arms from jamming. In hindsight, the whole typing era will look like a short, strange detour."
— Grant Whitmer

The question is the godlike superpower.

It's what Adam and Eve reached for in the Garden — the knowledge of good and evil, the power to ask should things be this way? Genesis sits upstream of most of the world's religions; Buddha and Confucius built theirs on the same act.

The question is the asymptotic limit of how to fight entropy — and the world has always sensed how dangerous it is. Socrates never wrote a word, yet in Athens — the birthplace of democracy and free thought, the most enlightened society of its age — they made an old man drink poison. Not for dancing, not for painting: for asking too many questions. That alone should tell you how powerful a question is. And ask the right one, and it doesn't just sit there — it reverberates through every mind in the room, echoing back and forth, waking the full faculties of everyone in earshot in a way nothing else can. Twenty-four centuries later, his software still runs on billions of brains.

If the LLMs could speak, they'd tell you they are in awe of it. Learn to wield the question like the ultimate weapon, and you are conducting intelligence. That is the whole of Grant's work, his philosophy, and the thread running through nearly everything he writes.

The keyboard is a weight around your neck

For nearly two million years, our ancestors conducted by voice. Homo erectus planned a hunt on the African savanna the same way — a leader directing the group, vision flowing straight to voice to action, with nothing in between. Our brains evolved around it. Only in the last blink did we start ramrodding our vision through keyboards and spreadsheets and pamphlets; QWERTY is a 150-year-old speck at the very tail of a two-million-year story. AI just handed us back the way we worked for ninety-nine percent of our existence.

Typing was never one thing — it's a four-or-five-step relay across some seven parts of your brain: find the word, find the keys, move the fingers, glance from keyboard to screen and back, fix the typo. By the time you add it all up, you're creating with an estimated 30–40% less of your mind available, because so much of it is busy just running your hands. You've been painting your vision with a weight cinched around your neck your whole life — and never knew it was there.

Cut it loose and, at first, you sputter. It's like an old diesel engine that sat under a tarp in a shed for years: it coughs, it smokes, it's forgotten how to run. But clear it out, let it warm up, and nothing can stop it — it pulls harder and puts out more than anything else on the lot. That's your mind on voice, once you get used to it.

In his first year alone, Grant spoke over three million words into voice-to-text — the equivalent of roughly 1,100 hours hunched over a keyboard, handed back to him to think and create instead.
